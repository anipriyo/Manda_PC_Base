<p align="center"><font size="7" color="#FF0000">WATER POLLUTION</font></p>
<p style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0); font-family: GeographEditWeb, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif; font-style: normal; font-variant-ligatures: normal; font-variant-caps: normal; font-weight: 400; letter-spacing: normal; orphans: 2; text-align: start; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0.8px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; text-decoration-thickness: initial; text-decoration-style: initial; text-decoration-color: initial; margin: 20px 0px; background-color: rgb(255, 255, 255)">
<font size="5">Some polluted water looks muddy, smells bad, and has garbage 
floating in it. Some polluted water looks clean, but is filled with harmful 
chemicals you can’t see or smell.<br>
<br>
Polluted water is unsafe for drinking and swimming. Some people who drink 
polluted water are exposed to hazardous chemicals that may make them sick years 
later. Others consume&nbsp;<a class="tipHelp" style="color: black !important; cursor: pointer; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 2px dotted rgb(51, 51, 51); background:">bacteria</a>&nbsp;and 
other tiny&nbsp;<a class="tipHelp" style="color: black !important; cursor: pointer; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 2px dotted rgb(51, 51, 51); background:">aquatic</a>&nbsp;organisms 
that cause disease. The&nbsp;<a class="tipHelp" style="color: black !important; cursor: pointer; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 2px dotted rgb(51, 51, 51); background:">United 
Nations</a>&nbsp;estimates that 4,000 children die every day from drinking dirty 
water.<br>
<br>
Sometimes, polluted water harms people indirectly. They get sick because the 
fish that live in polluted water are unsafe to eat. They have too many 
pollutants in their flesh.<br>
<br>
There are some natural sources of water pollution. Oil and natural gas, for 
example, can leak into oceans and lakes from natural underground sources. These 
sites are called&nbsp;<a class="tipHelp" style="color: black !important; cursor: pointer; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 2px dotted rgb(51, 51, 51); background:">petroleum 
seep</a>s. The world’s largest petroleum seep is the Coal Oil Point Seep, off 
the coast of the U.S. state of California. The Coal Oil Point Seep releases so 
much oil that&nbsp;<a class="tipHelp" style="color: black !important; cursor: pointer; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 2px dotted rgb(51, 51, 51); background:">tar 
ball</a>s wash up on nearby beaches. Tar balls are small, sticky pieces of 
pollution that eventually&nbsp;<a class="tipHelp" style="color: black !important; cursor: pointer; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 2px dotted rgb(51, 51, 51); background:">decompose</a>&nbsp;in 
the ocean.</font></p>
